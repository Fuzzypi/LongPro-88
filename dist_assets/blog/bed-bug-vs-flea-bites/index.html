Bed Bug vs. Flea Bites: How to Tell the Difference

April 29, 2026 by LongPro Pest Control

Bed bug and flea bites can look similar but the patterns, locations, and timing are different. A practical guide for Cleveland homeowners trying to figure out what's biting them.

You woke up with bites. They itch. You're not sure if it's bed bugs, fleas, mosquitoes, or something else. The treatment paths are very different depending on the answer, so it's worth taking a few minutes to figure out which is most likely before you panic-buy a $40 spray that doesn't work.

Here's how to read the difference between bed bug and flea bites — by location on your body, the pattern they form, and what you can find in the environment to confirm.

What the Bites Themselves Look Like

Bed bug and flea bites are honestly hard to tell apart from the bite alone. Both produce small, raised, itchy red welts that can last several days. Both can develop a darker red center. Skin reaction varies a lot from person to person — some people barely notice, others develop large itchy hives. Two people in the same household sleeping in the same infested bed can come away with totally different bite reactions.

This is why we don't recommend trying to identify the pest from the bite alone. The location and pattern of the bites, plus what you find in the environment, tell you much more than the welt itself.

Where the Bites Show Up Tells You a Lot

Bed bugs typically bite skin that's exposed while you sleep. Most often that means the upper body — face, neck, shoulders, arms, and upper back. Sometimes hands and lower legs. Bed bugs feed at night and crawl up to skin that's outside the covers, so anywhere your sleeping position exposes is fair game. They're not picky about which side of the body.

Fleas typically bite the lower body. Ankles, calves, lower legs are where most flea bites concentrate, because adult fleas live in carpets and pet bedding and jump up to bite as you walk through. If you find bites primarily below the knees and almost none on your upper body, fleas are far more likely than bed bugs.

Cluster of bites on the upper body, no bites below the waist? Bed bugs.

Cluster of bites on the ankles, none on the upper body? Fleas.

Bites all over including arms and legs but absent from sleeping areas? Mosquitoes are more likely.

The Pattern of Bites

Bed bug bites often show up in lines or small clusters of three, sometimes called the "breakfast, lunch, dinner" pattern. A single bed bug feeding gets disturbed and resumes feeding nearby, leaving multiple bite marks in a row along one area of skin. That linear or short-cluster pattern is one of the more recognizable signs of bed bugs.

Flea bites are typically scattered randomly across the skin in the bite zone, not in straight lines. Multiple fleas bite multiple places, and individual fleas tend to bite once and move rather than feeding repeatedly in a line.

When the Bites Happen

Bed bug bites happen during sleep. People wake up with bites that weren't there the night before. Bites that appear during the day while you're sitting on the couch or working in the office are unlikely to be bed bugs unless that couch or office is itself infested (which does happen — bed bugs do spread to upholstered work chairs).

Flea bites can happen anytime, especially when walking through carpeted areas where fleas live. If you walked across the family room and felt itchiness on your ankles within minutes, fleas are likely.

What to Look for in the Environment

Pest identification gets a lot easier when you stop looking at your skin and start looking at where you sleep, sit, and walk.

Signs of bed bugs: Dark spotting (digested-blood specks) on mattress seams, box spring fabric, bed frame joints, or bedding. Small white eggs glued into mattress seams. Shed exoskeletons (translucent skins) along baseboards or under the mattress. Live bugs in mattress folds, behind headboard, or inside outlet covers near the bed. The classic check: peel back the fitted sheet and inspect the four mattress corners with a flashlight.

Signs of fleas: Black pepper-like specks (flea "dirt," actually digested blood) on pet bedding, in carpet, or where pets sleep. Adult fleas jumping when you sit on the carpet (test: sit still on the floor for 60 seconds, then watch for tiny dark insects jumping near you). Pet scratching more than usual. Fleas cluster around pet sleep areas because that's where the eggs and larvae develop.

What to Do Next Based on What You Found

If the evidence points to bed bugs: Don't try to DIY-treat — consumer sprays don't kill eggs and the infestation will keep cycling. The fastest, cheapest path is a professional inspection. If the evidence points to fleas: Two things have to happen at once for flea control to work — treat the home, and treat the pet. Skipping either one means re-infestation within weeks. Coordinate with your veterinarian on a flea preventive for your pet, and have your home treated professionally if the infestation is more than a few bites. See our flea extermination service.

If you can't find environmental evidence of either: Other possibilities to consider include mosquitoes (especially if bites started after time outdoors at dawn or dusk), bird mites or rodent mites (if you have animal nests in the attic or walls), scabies (medical, see a doctor — these don't go away without prescription treatment), or skin reactions to laundry detergent, plant contact, or stress. When in doubt, a professional inspection rules pest causes in or out so you know whether to call us or call your doctor.

The good news about figuring out what's biting you: most cases are clear once you look at the location pattern and the environmental evidence together. The bad news: the longer you wait between noticing the bites and getting professional help, the harder both bed bug and flea infestations are to eliminate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How can I tell bed bug bites from flea bites just by looking at them? +

Honestly, you usually can't tell from the welts alone — they look very similar. The most reliable signals are location (bed bugs hit the upper body, fleas hit the ankles), pattern (bed bugs in lines or clusters of three, fleas scattered randomly), and environmental evidence (look at the mattress for bed bugs, the carpet and pet bedding for fleas).

Can you have bed bugs and fleas at the same time? +

Yes, though it's uncommon. If you have pets and you're seeing bites on both your ankles and your upper body in different patterns, you may be dealing with both. A professional inspection can confirm and the treatment plan will address each pest separately because the methods are different.

Do mosquito bites look like bed bug bites? +

Mosquito bites are usually larger, more uniformly round, and tend to appear on exposed skin during outdoor time at dawn or dusk. They typically itch immediately, while bed bug bites often itch most a day or two after the bite appears. Mosquito bites also rarely appear in clusters or straight lines.

How do I check for bed bugs vs. fleas in my home? +

For bed bugs, peel back the fitted sheet and inspect the mattress seams with a flashlight, looking for live bugs, dark blood spotting, shed skins, or small white eggs. For fleas, sit still on a carpeted floor for 60 seconds and watch for tiny dark insects jumping near you, or look at pet bedding for black pepper-like specks of flea dirt.

Should I get a professional inspection if I'm not sure what's biting me? +

Yes — that's exactly the situation a free inspection is for. A licensed technician can identify the pest from environmental evidence quickly, and even if the answer turns out to be "nothing pest-related," knowing that lets you stop chasing the wrong solution and see a doctor if that's the right next step.
