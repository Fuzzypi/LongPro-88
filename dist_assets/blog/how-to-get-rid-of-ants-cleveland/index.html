How to Get Rid of Ants in Your Cleveland Home

June 15, 2026 by LongPro Pest Control

Ants are the most common warm-weather pest complaint in Cleveland. Here is how to identify what you are dealing with, stop the trail, and know when it is time to call a professional.

Every summer, Cleveland homeowners deal with the same frustration: a line of ants marching across the kitchen counter, trailing along the bathroom baseboard, or clustering around the dog's food bowl. Ant activity in Northeast Ohio picks up sharply from late May through September, and once a colony finds a food or water source inside your home, the trail can be difficult to break without the right approach.

This guide covers the ant species most common in the Cleveland area, what draws them indoors, steps you can take on your own, and the signs that mean it is time for professional ant control.

Common Ant Species in Cleveland Homes

Not every ant responds to the same treatment. Identifying the species helps determine what is driving the activity and how to stop it.

Odorous House Ants

These are the most frequent indoor ant in Cleveland, Parma, Lakewood, and Brooklyn. They are small, dark brown to black, and give off a rotten coconut smell when crushed. They form large colonies with multiple queens and are strongly attracted to sweet foods and moisture. Odorous house ants nest in wall voids, under sinks, behind appliances, and beneath siding.

Pavement Ants

Pavement ants nest in cracks in driveways, sidewalks, and foundation slabs. You may notice small mounds of sand or soil near concrete joints. They enter homes through foundation cracks and expansion joints and are common in basements and ground-level apartments across Cuyahoga County.

Carpenter Ants

Carpenter ants are larger, usually black, and they excavate wood to build their nests. They do not eat wood like termites, but they hollow it out, which can cause structural damage over time. If you see large black ants indoors during winter or spring, or find small piles of sawdust-like frass near baseboards or window frames, carpenter ants may be nesting inside the structure. This species needs professional attention.

What Attracts Ants Into Cleveland Homes

Ants enter homes for three reasons: food, water, and shelter. In Cleveland's older housing stock, there are usually plenty of entry points. Common attractants include:

  • Crumbs and grease on counters, stovetops, and floors, especially around the kitchen.
  • Pet food left out in bowls on the floor.
  • Moisture from leaking pipes, condensation around windows, or standing water in basements.
  • Sweet residues from spills, recycling bins, or fruit left on counters.
  • Gaps around utility lines, doors, and windows that give ants a direct path from the exterior colony to an indoor food source.

The trail you see on the counter is not random. Scout ants lay a chemical trail for the rest of the colony to follow. Killing the visible ants without addressing the trail and the source usually results in them returning within hours or days.

Steps You Can Take on Your Own

For light ant activity that just started, these steps may be enough to stop it:

  • Clean the trail. Wipe down the path with soapy water or a vinegar solution to disrupt the pheromone trail. Clean the surrounding area thoroughly.
  • Remove the food source. Store food in sealed containers, clean grease from cooking surfaces, and keep pet food in sealed bins between feedings.
  • Fix moisture problems. Repair dripping faucets, tighten pipe joints under sinks, and use a dehumidifier in damp basements.
  • Seal entry points. Caulk cracks around windows, doors, pipes, and where utility lines enter the home. Replace damaged door sweeps and weatherstripping.
  • Use bait stations. Over-the-counter ant bait stations placed along the trail can work for small odorous house ant problems. Ants carry the bait back to the colony, which is more effective than contact sprays that only kill the ants you can see.

Avoid using repellent sprays near bait stations. Repellents can scatter the colony and cause it to split into multiple satellite nests, a process called budding. This makes the problem worse and harder to treat.

When to Call a Professional Exterminator

DIY measures work best for early, light activity. Call a professional when:

  • Ants keep coming back after cleaning and sealing, especially from the same area.
  • You see ants in multiple rooms or on multiple floors.
  • You find large black carpenter ants, particularly during winter or early spring when they should not be active indoors.
  • You notice sawdust piles or frass near wood surfaces.
  • The colony appears to be nesting inside a wall, cabinet, or under a slab.
  • You live in a multi-unit building in Cleveland, Lakewood, or Parma Heights where ants may be traveling between units through shared walls.

Professional ant control starts with identifying the species and finding the nest location or entry point. Treatment may include targeted baiting, crack-and-crevice application, perimeter treatment, or a combination depending on the species and severity. For carpenter ants, locating and treating the nest directly is essential to prevent wood damage.

Preventing Ants From Coming Back

After treatment, prevention is about making your home less attractive and harder to enter. The most effective long-term steps are:

  • Keep kitchens and dining areas clean daily, including under appliances.
  • Store all food, including pet food and birdseed, in sealed containers.
  • Trim tree branches and shrubs that touch the house. Ants use vegetation as a bridge to reach siding, soffits, and windows.
  • Move mulch and wood piles away from the foundation. Both attract ants and provide nesting material.
  • Address moisture issues promptly. Ants in Cleveland basements are almost always following water.
  • Schedule seasonal inspections if you have had carpenter ant activity in the past.
