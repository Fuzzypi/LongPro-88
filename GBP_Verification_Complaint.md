## Summary of Complaint

I am filing this complaint against Google LLC regarding its mandatory video verification requirement for Google Business Profile (GBP), a free business listing service operated by Google. The video verification requirement constitutes an invasive, disproportionate, and insufficiently disclosed data collection practice that harms legitimate small business owners while providing no meaningful proof of business legitimacy.

---

## Background

LongPro Pest Control LLC is a licensed pest control company based in Cleveland, Ohio, with over 13 years of continuous operation. The business holds an A+ rating with the Better Business Bureau, maintains a 5.0-star Google rating from verified customer reviews, and has served the Greater Cleveland area since 2011.

In May 2026, I attempted to claim and verify my business listing on Google Business Profile — a platform that Google has made effectively essential for local business visibility in Search and Maps. Upon completing the profile setup, Google presented video submission as the only available verification method.

---

## Grounds for Complaint

**1. Video verification does not establish business ownership or legitimacy.**

Google requires business owners to submit video footage as a condition of accessing their own business listing. However, submitting a video of supplies, equipment, or surroundings does not — and cannot — verify that a person owns or operates a business. A fraudulent actor could film the same items just as easily as a legitimate operator. The stated purpose of the requirement (fraud prevention) is not served by the method Google has chosen. Meanwhile, legitimate business owners with verifiable track records — licenses, tax records, BBB accreditation, customer reviews — are subjected to the same invasive requirement as anyone else, with no alternative.

**2. Google is collecting personal video data under the guise of business verification, with no clear disclosure of how it is stored, used, or shared.**

Google does not clearly disclose, at the point of video submission:
- How long the video footage is retained
- Whether the footage is reviewed by human employees or AI systems
- Whether biometric data (faces, voices) captured in the video is processed or stored
- Whether the footage is used for any purpose beyond verification
- What rights the submitter retains over the footage

This lack of disclosure, combined with the mandatory nature of the requirement, raises serious concerns under consumer protection law and applicable data privacy standards.

**3. The requirement is coercive.**

Google Business Profile is not truly optional for small businesses. Appearing in Google Search and Google Maps is essential to local business competition in 2026. By gating access to this critical platform behind a video submission requirement — with no alternative — Google has effectively made invasive data collection a condition of participation in the local economy. This is a disproportionate burden placed on small business owners who have no meaningful alternative.

**4. The requirement disproportionately impacts legitimate small businesses.**

Large, chain, or franchised businesses with established digital footprints are far less likely to face this verification barrier. Independent small business owners — who may have limited online presence precisely because they are new to digital marketing — bear the full weight of this policy. My business has 13+ years of operation, a verified BBB A+ rating, documented customer reviews on Google's own platform, and a licensed business registered in the State of Ohio. None of this evidence was considered as an alternative to video submission.

---

## Relief Requested

I respectfully request that the relevant authority investigate Google LLC's video verification practices for Google Business Profile and consider the following actions:

1. Require Google to provide clear, plain-language disclosure of all data collection, retention, and processing practices associated with video verification submissions, prior to collection.

2. Require Google to offer meaningful alternative verification methods that do not involve video submission — such as postcard-by-mail, government business registration documents, or utility bill verification.

3. Investigate whether Google's video verification requirement constitutes an unfair or deceptive trade practice under applicable consumer protection statutes.

4. Require Google to delete any video data submitted by business owners who were not clearly informed of how that data would be used.
